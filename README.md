# The Problems Keep Spilling
A short story on modern day ghetto problems. Generated by Claude Sonnet 4.6. 
---------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The room smelled like concrete and old smoke. A single bulb hung overhead, throwing a pale circle of light that didn't quite reach the walls. John sat in the center of it, ankles lashed to the chair legs, wrists cinched behind his back. The other men in the room didn't speak. They stood at the edges, arms folded, watching.

The standing man peeled the gag from John's mouth and took a step back, giving him room. He held the cloth loosely at his side and waited.

"Why did your men fumigate that apartment complex," he said, "while people were still in it?"

John was quiet for a moment. Not the quiet of someone caught off guard — the quiet of someone carefully selecting. When he finally spoke, his voice had a flat, almost mechanical quality to it, like he was reading from something internal.

"They were nothing but minorities in there. Blacks, Asians, you name it." He paused. "Some of them were drug dealers too."

The standing man's expression shifted, eyebrows lifting with exaggerated surprise. "Really?"

John nodded, and there was something unsettling in how natural it looked — the nod of a man who believed he had just offered a perfectly satisfying explanation. "I even know a guy," he added, "who told me he got robbed senseless by one of the tenants there. Cleaned out completely."

The standing man said nothing for a moment. He turned the cloth over in his hands, thinking. Then he looked back at John.

"So you admit it. Your men fumigated the building knowing people were inside. And the fumigation would have hurt them." He paused. "Yes?"

John's jaw tightened slightly. "That's only if they did it to hurt people," he said. "Maybe they didn't know."

"Maybe they didn't know," the standing man repeated, as if testing the weight of it. Then he moved to the side of the room, picked up a folder from a folding table, and dropped it open in front of John. "Then explain why there are records showing your men received illegally obtained information on the tenants. Personal information. The kind that gets hacked by organized crime. Where did that come from?"

John looked at the folder without really looking at it. "How should I know that?" His voice carried a trace of indignation now, something almost wounded in it. "You're acting like I'm the one that runs everything."

Outside the building, the sun was going down. Orange light stretched long across the pavement and then slowly withdrew, and the sky above it deepened into something darker.
